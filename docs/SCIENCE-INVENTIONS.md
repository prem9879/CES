# Science Inventions

This document defines two invention tracks designed to be testable, patentable, and deployment-oriented.

## 1) Living Thermal Battery Concrete (LTBC)

### Concept
A cementitious composite that stores heat as reversible thermochemical energy using embedded hydrate-salt microcapsules. The material charges during warm periods (dehydration) and discharges during cooler periods (rehydration heat release).

### Candidate chemistry
- Calcium chloride hydrate blends for high heat release density.
- Magnesium sulfate hydrate blends for cycle stability.
- Composite buffers to control volume change and capsule stress.

### Layer architecture
- Structural matrix with tuned thermal conductivity.
- Thermochemical capsule layer for charge-discharge function.
- Moisture-gate microchannel network for release timing.
- Surface vapor control to prevent uncontrolled ambient triggering.

### Validation plan (Phase 1)
- Build 9 lab tile variants across chemistry and capsule loading.
- Run 200 thermal charge-discharge cycles.
- Measure effective heat storage, discharge curve, and mechanical strength retention.
- Compare against PCM concrete control samples.

### Success criteria
- At least 2x usable overnight heat release versus PCM control at equal thickness.
- Less than 15 percent compressive strength reduction versus plain control mix.
- Less than 10 percent storage fade after 200 cycles.

### Technical risks
- Capsule leakage under compressive stress.
- Expansion-induced microcracking.
- Moisture drift reducing controllability.
- Freeze-thaw durability effects.

### Mitigations
- Multi-shell mineral capsule coatings.
- Expansion buffer pores in matrix design.
- Diffusion barriers and tuned pore structure.
- Climate-specific chemistry profile selection.

## 2) Spectral Fog-to-Water Metamaterial Skin (SFMS)

### Concept
A passive water-harvesting skin that combines infrared radiative cooling and directional wetting channels to convert humid air and fog into collected liquid water.

### Physical mechanism
- High sky-window emissivity drives surface cooling below ambient.
- Surface reaches or crosses dew point to nucleate droplets.
- Fog droplets are captured by texture gradients.
- Asymmetric wettability channels move water to a collector edge.

### Material stack
- Top texture layer with mixed hydrophilic nucleation islands and low-adhesion transport lanes.
- Infrared emissive backing for radiative cooling gain.
- Thermal isolation spacer to reduce parasitic heat backflow.
- Edge manifold for gravity-fed storage.

### Validation plan (Phase 1)
- Fabricate 5 texture families with 3 emissive coatings each.
- Test in controlled chamber at RH bands: 50, 70, 90 percent.
- Evaluate fog and dew modes separately, then combined.
- Measure net water yield, start time, and contamination load.

### Success criteria
- Measurable water yield in both fog mode and dew mode.
- At least 30 percent yield improvement over single-mode fog mesh controls.
- Stable performance across 60+ day outdoor pilot exposure.

### Technical risks
- Dust and biofouling reducing wettability control.
- Surface aging degrading emissivity.
- Wind shear lowering droplet retention.
- Water quality contamination from surface chemistry.

### Mitigations
- Self-cleaning microtopography and periodic rinse protocol.
- UV-stable emissive polymer system.
- Wind-baffle geometry near collection edge.
- Food-safe surface chemistry and post-collection filtration.

## IP and commercialization notes
- Protect architecture claims, material formulation claims, and process claims separately.
- Run novelty search before public disclosure.
- Pilot in one climate and one controlled built environment before scaling.
